### Addendum — give this to Cursor alongside the other 3 documents.

## THE ACTUAL PROBLEM
The environment images (bright street, dusk street, dark rail yard, dark station) are **mood/style references for what the world should look like** — they are not meant to BE the level. Right now the game likely treats one image as the entire level (a single static screen you move around inside). That's why it feels like a short box instead of a real place.

What you actually want: a **long, connected map** — straight stretches, junctions where the path turns left/right, wider open areas, and enclosed rooms/alcoves you can duck into — built from small reusable pieces, not one big picture.

This is exactly how real top-down games (and Killer Bean's levels) are built: a **tile/module system**, not a single background image.

---

## HOW THIS ACTUALLY WORKS — MODULAR LEVEL SYSTEM

Instead of one background image per level, the level is assembled from a set of reusable **segment pieces**, each representing a chunk of the world:

- **Straight corridor segment** — a stretch of road/hallway, obstacles along the sides
- **Turn segment** (left and right variants) — the path bends 90 degrees
- **Junction segment** — path splits (left/right/straight), forces a real navigation choice
- **Open area segment** — wider space, more agents, more cover options, higher risk/reward
- **Room segment** — an enclosed side-space off the main path, usually containing either a shortcut, a cloak pickup, or nothing (a decoy) — this is what gives you actual rooms to hide in, not just corridor-hugging
- **Dead-end/alcove segment** — a small pocket built purely for hiding, no through-path, just cover

Each segment is a fixed-size chunk (e.g. one "screen height" tall) with defined connection points (where it can attach to the next segment) and its own obstacle/cover layout. A level is generated by **chaining segments together** — either hand-placed by you for a designed level, or randomly assembled from the segment pool for procedural variety between playthroughs.

**The camera follows the player through this chain** — the world scrolls/pans as you move, rather than the player being boxed into one static screen. This is the single biggest change from what exists now.

### How the reference images fit in
Your 4 environment images are **texture/mood references for the art style of each biome tier**, not literal level maps. The actual level segments should be built as smaller modular art pieces (road textures, wall/building-front tiles, rubble/crate obstacle sprites, container/rail-yard obstacle sprites) that share the *palette and lighting mood* of those reference images, repeated and combined to build the long connected map. Think of the reference images as "here's the vibe," and the segment tiles as "here's the actual Lego bricks that vibe is built from."

## WHAT TO TELL CURSOR, TECHNICALLY

1. **Replace the single fixed-background level with a segment-based world.** Define a `LevelSegment` type: id, biome tier, size, entry/exit connection points, obstacle layout, agent spawn points, optional room/alcove sub-areas.
2. **Camera becomes a scrolling/following camera**, not a fixed viewport — it tracks the player's position with some lead/lag, revealing new segments as they're approached and culling ones far behind for performance.
3. **Level generation chains segments**: start segment → N corridor/turn/junction/room segments → exit/portal segment. Difficulty scaling (from the original brief) now also controls segment variety — early levels use mostly straight corridors with occasional turns, later levels introduce junctions, rooms, and dead-ends that demand real route decisions, not just reflexes.
4. **Rooms/alcoves must be genuinely worth detouring into** — place cloak pickups, shortcuts, or safer alternate paths there, not nothing, or players will learn to ignore them and the "explore for cover" feeling you want disappears.
5. **Obstacle/cover placement should support the physical hiding mechanic from the realism-fix document** — corners and doorway edges are natural cover points; make sure segment art includes enough wall-corners and pillars/crates positioned so players can round a corner and be genuinely out of an agent's sightline, not just standing in open space with lower exposure numbers.

## PRACTICAL NEXT STEP FOR ART
You don't need to redraw your 4 environment images — keep them as style/mood reference. What Cursor needs from you (or what you generate next) is a smaller set of **tileable pieces** per biome tier:
- Ground/road texture (tileable)
- 2-3 wall/building-front pieces (tileable or capped-end variants)
- 4-6 obstacle/cover objects per biome (already partially covered by your crate/rubble/container assets)
- Optional: a distinct "room floor" texture so alcoves read as a different space than the main path

This is a much smaller, more reusable art set than 4 one-off full-level paintings — and it's what actually lets the world feel long and explorable instead of boxed.
